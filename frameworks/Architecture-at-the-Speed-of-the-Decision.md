# Architecture at the Speed of the Decision
## The Governing Argument of the Velocity Architecture Framework™

**Author:** ZenCloud Global Consultants  
**Framework:** Velocity Architecture Framework™ (VAF)  
**Published:** May 2026

---

## The position

Most EA frameworks are sequenced incorrectly.

The standard sequence runs: assess the current state → produce artefacts → have conversations → make decisions.

The sequence that works in practice runs: have the decision conversation → identify what needs to be recorded → produce the minimum artefact that captures the commitment.

This is not a critique of EA frameworks. TOGAF Phase A puts Architecture Vision first — the conversation before the deliverables. The problem is not the framework. It is how practitioners have interpreted it. The artefact has become the goal. The decision has become the by-product.

Architecture at the speed of the decision reverses that. The decision is the goal. The artefact is the record.

---

## Why this matters

Decisions happen at the speed the organisation is moving. In a large, stable government department with a three-year programme, that speed is compatible with a twelve-week capability mapping exercise. In a Series B technology company, a growing media enterprise, or a public sector organisation mid-transformation, it is not.

The practitioner who cannot produce architectural thinking at the speed decisions are being made is not present when decisions are made. They arrive after the fact with a comprehensive document that ratifies something already decided, or challenges something already committed. Neither position is architecture. Both are overhead.

The question the Velocity Architecture Framework™ is built to answer is: what is the minimum coherent architectural output that serves this decision, at the resolution this decision requires, in the time this decision is being made?

## The empirical basis

Kotusev's analysis of EA artefact usage across 47 diverse organisations found that most significant problems in EA practice arise because the usage of individual EA artefacts in practice is poorly understood. Most organisations do not follow EA methodologies and frameworks strictly — they adapt them to their needs or use them only as idea contributors.

The organisations that succeed are the ones that have understood, implicitly or explicitly, that the artefact exists to serve the decision — not the other way around.

CB Insights' analysis of 431 failed VC-backed companies (2024) found that 43% failed due to poor product-market fit — the problem of building without validating. The same failure mode operates at the organisational architecture level: organisations invest without capability validation, decommission without integration mapping, transform without process baseline. The artefact set exists to prevent exactly those failures. But only if the artefact is produced at the moment the decision requires it — not after the decision has already been made by default.

Koning, Hasan & Chatterji (Management Science, 2022) demonstrated across 35,000 startups that iterative validation discipline — producing the minimum evidence required to make a decision before committing — improves performance 30–100% within a year. The mechanism is not the specific validation instrument. It is the discipline of validating before committing. That is the mechanism VAF applies to architectural decision-making.

## What changes in practice

**The sequence changes.** The conversation happens first. The artefact is produced after it, as the record. Not before it, as the preparation.

**The scope changes.** The artefact is scoped to the decision it serves. A Business Capability Map produced to support an investment decision between two capability areas does not require a full L1/L2/L3 taxonomy. It requires the heat map at the resolution that makes the investment gap visible. That is the scope. Stop there.

**The timeline changes.** An Architecture Vision produced in one structured session with the output written immediately after is not a lesser artefact than one produced over eight weeks with stakeholder workshops and approval cycles. It is a more honest one. It records what was actually committed to — not what a process produced.

**The maintenance obligation changes.** When the artefact is understood as a record of a decision, the maintenance trigger is obvious: when the decision context changes, the record must be updated. Annual review cycles are replaced by condition-based refresh triggers named at the time the artefact is produced.

---

## What does not change

The rigour of the underlying analysis does not change. The discipline of the extraction conversation — asking the right questions, recording what was actually said, naming the things that were not agreed as explicitly as the things that were — is as demanding as any workshop-based methodology. More demanding, in some respects, because there is no volume of documentation to hide behind.

The accountability does not change. Every artefact produced under the VAF framework has a named decision owner, a named date, and a named invalidation trigger. The compression removes overhead — not accountability.

The evidence base does not change. The research papers in this repository are sourced, graded, and honest about their limitations. The VAF framework is not asserted to be empirically validated at the artefact level — because that study does not exist. What is asserted is that the compression logic is derived from what the research supports, applied to what practitioners know works in the room.

## The practitioner implication

If you are an architect reading this, the implication is simple: your value is in the conversation, not the document.

The executive you are trying to influence does not read the BCM. They react to the conversation where you show them, specifically and briefly, where investment is misaligned with priority. The BCM is the preparation for that conversation and the record after it. It is not the deliverable.

If you can run that conversation without twelve weeks of preparation, you are present when the decision is being made. That is the only position from which architecture influences outcomes.

Architecture at the speed of the decision is not a shortcut. It is the correct practice. The twelve-week version is the shortcut — it replaces the hard work of being in the right conversation at the right time with the easier work of producing a comprehensive document that arrives too late.

---

## The organisational implication

If you are a leader in an organisation without a dedicated EA function, the implication is equally simple: the decisions you are making right now without architectural input are producing the technical debt, integration failures, and capability gaps you will spend the next three years trying to resolve.

You do not need to hire an enterprise architect to change that. You need one person willing to run the conversations this toolkit describes, write down what was agreed, and bring the finding to the person who needs to act on it.

That is architecture. It has always been architecture. The frameworks and the artefacts are instruments in service of that practice — not the practice itself.
