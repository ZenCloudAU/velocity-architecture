# Architecture as a Decision System

The problem was never the artefact. It was always the gap between what was written and what was real.

## LinkedIn Excerpt

There is a version of this argument you have probably heard before.
Documentation is dead. Principles do not govern. Move to executable constraints. Embed guardrails in pipelines. Let the platform enforce what the document never could.

That is not the argument I am making.

The argument I am making is different — and I think more honest.

Artefacts are not the problem. Dead artefacts are.

A dead artefact is written after the decision was already made. It reflects what the team agreed to say, not what they actually decided. It satisfies a governance ritual and is outdated before the review cycle closes. Nobody reads it. Nobody updates it. It sits in a repository accumulating version numbers while the system it describes moves on without it.

This is the real failure. Not documentation. Not rigour. Not the artefact itself.

The failure is the gap — between what is written and what is real, between the moment a decision is made and the moment it is recorded, between the system as it runs and the architecture as it is documented.

Close that gap and the artefact becomes the most powerful governance tool available. Leave it open and no amount of documentation produces coherence.

Architecture as a decision system is about closing the gap. In every industry. At every scale.
