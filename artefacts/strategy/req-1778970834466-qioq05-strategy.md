## Context

An Australian healthcare NGO operates across **200 sites** serving a **20,000-user base** distributed nationally. The current infrastructure estate runs on **aging VMware platforms** spread across **multiple data centres** that have accumulated organically over time — a pattern typical of organisations that grew through service expansion, merger, and grant-funded capital investment without a unifying architecture mandate.

The organisation now faces a converging set of pressures:

- **VMware licensing and support costs** have increased materially following Broadcom's acquisition, eliminating the historical cost stability that made VMware the default platform choice
- **Aging hardware** in multiple DC locations is approaching end of support, creating compounding capital refresh cycles with no consolidation dividend
- **Regulatory exposure** under the Australian Privacy Act, the *My Health Records Act 2012*, and applicable ACSC Essential Eight controls requires demonstrable data sovereignty, access governance, and incident response capability — all of which are harder to sustain across a fragmented multi-DC estate
- **Operational complexity** at 200 sites with heterogeneous infrastructure creates high mean-time-to-resolution, disproportionate support cost per site, and limited capacity for proactive security posture

The strategic direction is to **consolidate to 2–3 geographically distributed, purpose-designed hosting nodes** aligned to Australian health data sovereignty requirements, while establishing a **capability model** that governs how compute, storage, networking, and clinical application services are delivered to the full user base going forward.

This decision record addresses which **capability model** — the architectural pattern for how infrastructure capabilities are organised, owned, and delivered — will govern the consolidation programme. Platform selection (hypervisor, cloud, hybrid) is a downstream decision that flows from this commitment.

The **Integrity Gap** in this environment is significant. With multiple DCs, aging VMware builds, and no consolidated capability register, the documented state of the infrastructure almost certainly diverges from operational reality. The capability model chosen must produce a **Velocity of Truth** mechanism — a means by which the organisation can close that gap and sustain an accurate, current understanding of what it runs, where, and why.

## Options Considered

| Option | Summary | Key Advantage | Key Risk |
|--------|---------|---------------|----------|
| **A — Federated Capability Model** | Retain distributed ownership; each DC operates its own capability stack with loose coordination standards | Minimal disruption to existing teams; preserves local autonomy | Perpetuates the Integrity Gap; does not achieve consolidation dividend; compliance posture remains fragmented |
| **B — Centralised Managed Capability Model** | All infrastructure capability centralised to 2–3 nodes under unified governance; sites become thin consumers | Maximum standardisation; strongest compliance posture; lowest long-term operational cost | High transition complexity; significant change management burden; risk of over-engineering for small-site needs |
| **C — Zoned Capability Model (Hybrid Consolidation)** | Capabilities consolidated to 2–3 geo-nodes operating as authoritative zones; sites retain minimal local resilience only where clinically justified | Balances consolidation with clinical continuity requirements; aligns to health sector patterns; scoped compliance surface | Requires disciplined zone boundary governance; partial complexity retained at site tier |
| **D — Cloud-First Capability Model** | Decommission owned DC estate entirely; migrate all workloads to sovereign-compliant cloud (Azure Australia, AWS AP-Southeast) | Eliminates capital refresh cycle; highest elasticity | Health data sovereignty constraints limit feasible workload set; latency risk for clinical applications at remote sites; organisational readiness low |

## Decision Drivers

| Driver | Weight | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| **Regulatory compliance** (Privacy Act, My Health Records, ACSC Essential Eight) | Critical | Non-negotiable. Any capability model that cannot demonstrate a defensible compliance posture is disqualified regardless of cost or efficiency |
| **Clinical continuity** | Critical | 200 sites include clinical service delivery points. Infrastructure failure must not translate to clinical service failure. Model must support defined RTO/RPO for clinical workloads |
| **Data sovereignty** | High | Health records must remain within Australian jurisdiction. Offshore processing or replication — even transient — creates regulatory exposure |
| **Operational sustainability** | High | Current model is not sustainable. 200 sites with heterogeneous VMware builds cannot be maintained at current staffing levels without continued risk accumulation |
| **Cost efficiency** | High | The organisation operates under grant and service-funding constraints. Capital expenditure must be justified across a multi-year envelope; operational expenditure reduction is a programme goal, not a target to optimise prematurely |
| **Team capability and transition risk** | Medium | Existing infrastructure team has VMware depth. Capability model must not assume skills the organisation does not yet possess without a credible uplift pathway |
| **Delivery speed** | Medium | Decision latency is already a risk. The longer the current fragmented estate runs, the higher the probability of a compliance or availability incident that forces a reactive rather than deliberate transition |

## Decision

**Option C — Zoned Capability Model (Hybrid Consolidation) is the committed direction.**

The organisation will establish **2–3 geographically distributed capability zones** across Australia — each operating as an authoritative, fully governed infrastructure node. Zone locations will be selected based on:

1. Proximity to the highest-density site clusters (minimising latency for clinical applications)
2. Alignment with jurisdictional health data requirements (no zone outside Australian territory)
3. Carrier and connectivity diversity (no single network dependency across zones)

All sites in the 200-site estate will be **reclassified as zone consumers**, not infrastructure operators. Sites will retain only what is clinically justified as local — specifically: local network switching, clinical device connectivity, and a defined minimum local caching capability for applications where WAN dependency creates patient safety risk. All compute, storage, identity, security, and application hosting capability moves to the zones.

VMware will be **retained as the hypervisor platform for the transition period** (estimated 18–24 months) on the basis that team capability is established, migration risk is bounded, and the alternative hypervisor evaluation (a downstream decision) must not block consolidation commencement. This is an explicit, conscious trade-off. The programme will not wait for hypervisor resolution before beginning DC consolidation.

A **Capability Register** — a live, continuously reconciled record of all infrastructure capabilities, their zone assignment, their governing compliance controls, and their operational owners — will be established as a programme artefact in the first 90 days. This is the primary instrument for closing the Integrity Gap and sustaining the Velocity of Truth across the programme lifecycle.

**The analytic test is met:** A delivery team can begin zone design, site classification, and capability register initialisation the day this decision is ratified. No further interpretation is required.

## Sequencing Rationale

Architecture programmes fail most often not because the wrong option is chosen, but because the sequence of decisions is wrong — later decisions are made before the earlier ones that should constrain them. This programme has a defined decision sequence:

### Phase 1 — Establish the Capability Foundation (Months 1–3)
*Decisions that must be made before anything else can be designed.*

1. **Ratify this Trade-off Matrix** — the capability model is the governing frame for all downstream decisions
2. **Zone location selection** — identify 2–3 specific hosting nodes (owned DC, colocation, or sovereign cloud anchor); this decision must precede hardware procurement or lease commitment
3. **Capability Register initialisation** — enumerate all current capabilities, their locations, their owners, and their compliance obligations; this is the baseline without which migration sequencing cannot be done safely
4. **Site classification** — categorise all 200 sites by clinical dependency profile, connectivity quality, and local infrastructure footprint; this drives migration wave sequencing

### Phase 2 — Zone Build and Governance (Months 3–12)
*Infrastructure construction proceeds only after Phase 1 is complete.*

5. **Hypervisor platform decision** — evaluate VMware continuation vs. alternative (Nutanix, Proxmox, or sovereign-compliant cloud IaaS) now that zone architecture is defined; this decision is deliberately deferred from Phase 1 to prevent it blocking consolidation commencement
6. **Zone network architecture** — inter-zone replication, WAN aggregation, SD-WAN for site connectivity, ACSC Essential Eight network controls
7. **Identity and access consolidation** — single identity plane across all zones; prerequisite for clinical application governance and audit trail compliance
8. **Security operations model** — SIEM, SOC alignment, incident response playbook across zone topology

### Phase 3 — Migration Execution (Months 9–30)
*Migration waves sequenced by site risk profile, not by convenience.*

9. **Wave 1 — Low-complexity, non-clinical administrative sites** (proof of pattern, lowest risk)
10. **Wave 2 — Mixed-use sites with clinical workloads** (pattern validated, clinical continuity protocols active)
11. **Wave 3 — High-dependency clinical sites** (full clinical continuity controls, local minimum retained per classification)
12. **DC decommission** — only after 100% workload migration validated and 90-day stabilisation period cleared

**Decision Latency Risk:** The organisation has been operating the current fragmented estate without a binding architectural direction. Every month this decision is not made, the VMware licensing liability accumulates, the hardware refresh backlog grows, and the compliance exposure widens. The 14-Day Decision Aging Rule (CR-E1) applies from the date this artefact is circulated. If this Trade-off Matrix has not been ratified within 14 calendar days of circulation, escalation is mandatory.

## Trade-offs Accepted

### What is gained

| Gain | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Compliance posture** | A zoned, consolidated model produces a bounded, auditable compliance surface. ACSC Essential Eight controls, data sovereignty, and privacy obligations become tractable to govern and evidence |
| **Operational sustainability** | A small, capable infrastructure team operating 2–3 well-governed zones is more sustainable than the same team managing 200 site variations |
| **Consolidation dividend** | Hardware refresh, licensing, and operational costs concentrate rather than multiply; long-term cost envelope reduces materially |
| **Velocity of Truth** | The Capability Register closes the Integrity Gap. The organisation will, for the first time, have an accurate and current picture of what it operates |
| **Delivery confidence** | Zones are a pattern the industry understands. Delivery partners, colocation providers, and clinical application vendors all operate within this model |

### What is accepted as cost

| Cost | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Transition complexity** | Moving 200 sites through a zoned migration is a multi-year programme requiring sustained programme governance, change management, and clinical risk management |
| **VMware lock-in — transition period** | Retaining VMware for 18–24 months accepts continued Broadcom licensing cost exposure. This is the price of not allowing hypervisor uncertainty to block consolidation commencement |
| **Local autonomy reduction** | Site staff and local IT contacts lose infrastructure autonomy. This is a deliberate architectural choice — it is not a risk to be mitigated, it is a consequence to be managed through change leadership |
| **Short-term capital commitment** | Zone build requires upfront capital before the consolidation dividend is realised. The payback period is estimated at 3–4 years from zone completion |
| **Governance overhead** | A Capability Register and zone governance model require ongoing discipline. Organisations that do not maintain this discipline will rebuild the Integrity Gap within 18 months of programme completion |

## Investment Profile

| Phase | Estimated Envelope | Primary Cost Drivers | Benefit Realisation |
|-------|--------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Phase 1 — Foundation** (Months 1–3) | Low–Medium | Architecture and programme governance; discovery and Capability Register tooling; zone location assessment | Compliance posture clarity; baseline established |
| **Phase 2 — Zone Build** (Months 3–12) | High | Colocation or DC build; compute and storage hardware or IaaS commitment; network uplift; security tooling | Zones operational; first migration waves unblocked |
| **Phase 3 — Migration** (Months 9–30) | Medium–High | Migration execution; site connectivity uplift; application re-hosting; decommission costs | Per-wave operational cost reduction; DC lease/power savings realised progressively |
| **Steady State** (Post Month 30) | Low–Medium (ongoing Opex) | Zone operations; licensing (post-VMware decision); support | Full consolidation dividend; materially reduced risk profile |

*Note: Precise figures require zone location selection and hardware/colocation market engagement. These are intentional programme activities in Phase 1, Weeks 4–8. Investment envelope quantification must not become a prerequisite for committing to the capability model — that sequencing error would constitute Decision Latency.*
