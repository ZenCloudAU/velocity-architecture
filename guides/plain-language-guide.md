# Plain Language Guide
## Start here if you have never hired an enterprise architect

**Who this is for:** Small and mid-size organisations, delivery teams, founders, operations leads, and anyone who needs to make better decisions about technology and capability — without a six-figure consulting engagement or a dedicated architecture function.

**What this is not:** A framework compliance guide. A TOGAF reference. A consulting sales document.

---

## The one thing you need to understand

Large organisations hire enterprise architects. Small organisations do not. But the decisions that enterprise architects exist to support — where to invest, what to build, what to stop doing, how systems connect, who owns what — happen in every organisation regardless of size.

When those decisions happen without structure, they get made on gut feel, seniority, or whoever is loudest in the room. Sometimes that works. More often it produces the thing you are probably already experiencing: technology investments that do not connect to strategic priorities, systems that cannot talk to each other, no-one who can say with confidence what the organisation actually has and what it costs.

Enterprise architecture exists to make those decisions deliberately. The Velocity Architecture Framework (VAF) exists to make that possible without the overhead that makes conventional EA inaccessible to most organisations.

---

## What an EA artefact actually is

An artefact is a document that serves a specific purpose in a decision or governance process. The word sounds complicated. The thing itself is not.

A business capability map is a list of what your organisation needs to be able to do, marked up against how much you are investing in each thing. That is it. It surfaces where you are over-investing in things that do not matter and under-investing in things that do.

An architecture decision record is a note written immediately after a significant decision that says: what was decided, what was considered and rejected, and what would change this decision. Three fields. Written the same day.

An application portfolio is a list of your technology systems, what they cost, who owns them, and whether they support something strategically important. It forces one classification per system: keep it, invest in it, move away from it, or shut it down.

None of these are complicated. What makes them hard is not the content — it is the conversation required to surface the content. That is what this toolkit addresses.

## The problem with conventional EA

Conventional EA frameworks (TOGAF is the most widely used) were designed for large organisations with dedicated architecture teams, multi-year programmes, and the resources to produce comprehensive documentation.

They produce artefacts that are complete, rigorous, and — in most organisations below a certain size — never used for anything.

A business capability map that takes twelve weeks to produce in an organisation that needs to make an investment decision in two weeks is not a useful instrument. It is overhead.

The VAF approach compresses the artefact to the resolution the decision requires. Not less. Not more.

---

## The four types of artefact — and why the type matters

Every EA artefact resolves to one of four functions. Understanding which function an artefact serves tells you what it needs to contain and how to maintain it.

**Decision instruments** force a commitment and shift the burden of proof. Examples: Architecture Vision, Application Portfolio (TIME classification), Technology Roadmap. These are front-loaded — their value is highest at the point of commitment and decays as context changes. If a decision instrument does not produce a decision, it is a slide deck.

**Control mechanisms** operate without authority by requiring justification for deviation. Examples: Architecture Principles, Technology Standards, Compliance Review. These accumulate value over time as the exception record builds. The list is not the mechanism — the exception process is.

**Signal generators** make invisible problems undeniable. Examples: Business Capability Map, Value Stream Map, Organisation Map. Their value is highest at delivery. Without a structural response, a signal generator becomes evidence of an acknowledged problem that was not addressed — which is worse than no artefact.

**Ground truth artefacts** are accuracy-dependent. Examples: Integration Architecture, Infrastructure Architecture, Architecture Decision Records. These bifurcate: maintained artefacts hold value indefinitely; unmaintained ones decay at the rate of organisational change.

## How to use the VAF toolkit — in plain steps

**Step 1: Identify the decision in front of you.**

Not the artefact. The decision. What are you trying to get agreed, funded, or stopped? Start there.

**Step 2: Find the artefact type that serves that decision.**

- Trying to get investment approved? You need a decision instrument — probably a Business Capability Map or an Application Portfolio classification.
- Trying to stop bad technology choices being made without review? You need a control mechanism — probably Architecture Principles with an exception process.
- Trying to show leadership where the organisation is broken? You need a signal generator — probably a Value Stream Map or an Organisation Map.
- Trying to protect a past decision from being relitigated? You need a ground truth artefact — an Architecture Decision Record.

**Step 3: Run the extraction conversation.**

Every artefact in this toolkit comes with an extraction guide — the questions to ask, the facilitation method, and the red flags that tell you when you are getting the policy version instead of the real version. The content of the artefact comes from the conversation. You cannot produce a useful artefact from a desk.

**Step 4: Complete the VAF template.**

Minimum fields only. The guidance tells you what to write and at what resolution. If you are adding sections not in the template, ask whether they serve the decision or serve the appearance of rigour.

**Step 5: Frame the finding for the person who needs to act on it.**

Every artefact section in the Practitioner Operating Manual includes an executive framing guide: the format, the presentation structure, what never to say, and a template sentence. The artefact is the evidence. The framing is the recommendation. They are different things and they are presented differently.

## What you do not need

You do not need a dedicated enterprise architect to use this toolkit. You need one person willing to run the conversations, complete the templates, and present the findings. That person does not need a framework certification. They need curiosity, the ability to ask direct questions, and the discipline to write things down the same day decisions are made.

You do not need specialist software. Every template in this toolkit works in a Word document, a Notion page, a Confluence space, or a shared Google Doc.

You do not need a programme to sequence the artefacts. You need the decision that is in front of you right now, and the artefact that serves it. Start with one. Complete it properly. The next one will be obvious.

---

## The one rule

Write the artefact after the conversation. Not before it.

The artefact is the record of what was agreed, what was discovered, what was decided, or what was found. It is not the preparation material for the conversation. If you are producing an artefact to justify a conversation you have not had yet, you are producing overhead.

Have the conversation. Write what happened. That is architecture.
