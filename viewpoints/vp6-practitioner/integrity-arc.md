## The Five Positions

### Position 1: Authorship

The practitioner is asserting intent into the system — not facilitating, not documenting, but saying: *here is the shape of the decision, and here is why.*

**Indicators that authorship is present:**
- Recommendations are stated, not offered neutrally
- Trade-offs are named and owned, not listed without conclusion
- The practitioner will defend the position when challenged
- Delivery can act on the output without interpretation

**Indicators that authorship is absent:**
- Documents describe options without committing to one
- Recommendations are qualified to the point of meaninglessness
- The practitioner is described as "keeping things moving" or "holding the space"
- Output could be read as supporting multiple conflicting directions

### Position 2: Boundaries

The practitioner has declared — explicitly and in writing — what architecture is and is not accountable for in this engagement.

**A boundary declaration states:**
- What the practitioner will design, decide, and own
- What the practitioner will *not* own — and who must own it instead
- The conditions under which the practitioner's involvement has meaning
- The conditions under which it does not

**Indicators that boundaries are clear:**
- The practitioner can state their scope in two sentences
- Decisions belonging to other functions are returned, not absorbed
- There is a written record of what is and is not in scope

**Indicators that boundaries are absent:**
- The practitioner is absorbing decisions that belong elsewhere
- The scope expands in each meeting
- The practitioner's name appears on outcomes they cannot control

### Position 3: Refusal

The practitioner has identified work they will not do — specifically, work that would require converting indecision into artefacts.

**Refusal sounds like:**  
*"I can design within a decision, but I cannot replace one."*  
*"I can document what is known, but I will not document ambiguity as if it were direction."*  
*"This work cannot proceed because the condition required for it to have meaning is absent."*

**Refusal does not:**
- Argue or persuade
- Escalate emotionally
- Seek vindication
- Produce a counter-proposal that absorbs the same indecision in a different form

### Position 4: Staying

The practitioner has assessed the engagement and made a deliberate choice to remain — without illusion about what the system will or will not do.

**Staying with integrity means:**
- Not expecting that effort will convert to alignment
- Not framing organisational inertia as personal inadequacy
- Being selective about where work has leverage
- Not performing availability as a substitute for authority

**Staying without integrity looks like:**
- Continuing to produce artefacts knowing they will not be used
- Absorbing decisions that belong elsewhere to maintain engagement
- Describing ceremonial work as meaningful
- Hoping the system will change if the next document is good enough

### Position 5: Exit

Exit is the last architectural decision still available. It is reached when continued participation would require misrepresentation of progress, intent, or responsibility.

**Exit is appropriate when:**
- The conditions for meaningful architectural work are structurally absent
- Refusal has been exercised and absorbed without consequence
- Staying would require actively misrepresenting the state of the architecture
- The practitioner's presence is being used to provide cover for decisions that will not be made

**Exit is not:**
- Burnout (that is a signal, not the decision itself)
- Frustration with a difficult environment
- A response to being challenged or overruled
- Abandonment of the work

**A clean exit:**
- Leaves behind a clear record of what was surfaced, what was required, and what was not taken up
- Does not burn bridges or seek vindication
- Does not announce itself as protest
- Is not performative
