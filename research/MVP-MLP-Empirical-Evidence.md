# MVP and MLP — The Empirical Evidence
## What the Research Actually Supports

**Author:** ZenCloud Global Consultants  
**Published:** May 2026  
**Classification:** Research Analysis  
**Evidence standard:** Peer-reviewed sources distinguished from practitioner aggregations throughout

## Why this matters for EA

Both MVP and MLP are invoked in enterprise transformation contexts — particularly in justifying phased delivery approaches, validating capability investment, and structuring governance checkpoints. They are also frequently cited as equivalent evidence-backed approaches.

They are not equivalent. One has defensible empirical backing. One does not. The distinction matters when either concept is used to justify architecture investment decisions.

---

## The failure context

Before examining the approaches, the problem they address must be stated clearly.

**CB Insights, 2024 — 431 VC-backed companies analysed**

| Failure cause | % of failures | Classification |
|---|---|---|
| Capital depletion | 70% | Symptom — not root cause |
| Poor product-market fit | 43% | Root cause |
| Bad timing | 29% | Root cause |
| Wrong team | 23% | Root cause |
| Unsustainable unit economics | 19% | Root cause |

Capital depletion is where failure stories end. Poor product-market fit is where they begin. Running out of capital affected 70% of failures, but CB Insights explicitly identifies that as the final symptom — not the disease.

A 2024–25 study of startups found 67% of failures happened because they built products nobody wanted — not because of bad code or technical problems (Softermii, 2024).

Product failure rates by sector: consumer packaged goods 70–85% (Nielsen). PDMA research shows best-performing business units achieve 24% new product failure rate vs 46% for the rest — the separator is structured validation discipline.

## MVP — the evidence assessment

### What MVP claims

Minimum Viable Product: the smallest version of a product that enables a team to assess whether users will derive meaningful value, before investing in a full-scale solution. The build-measure-learn feedback loop (Ries, 2011) as the organising principle for development decision-making.

### The strongest evidence

**Koning, Hasan & Chatterji — Management Science, Vol. 68, No. 9, September 2022**

Study scope: approximately 35,000 global high-technology startups tracked over four years, exploiting the time-varying adoption of A/B testing technology as a natural experiment.

Key finding:

> "We find that, although relatively few firms adopt A/B testing, among those that do, performance improves by 30%–100% after a year of use."

Mechanism: experimentation improves organisational learning, which helps startups develop more new products, identify and scale promising ideas, and — critically — fail faster when they receive negative signals.

This is the closest thing in the published literature to hard empirical evidence that iterative validation discipline improves organisational performance. The specific instrument is A/B testing. The generalised mechanism is: validate before committing, and respond to negative signals quickly.

### Practitioner-cited statistics

The following are industry aggregations, not peer-reviewed findings. They are cited frequently and should be treated as practitioner evidence, not research evidence:

- MVP-based projects: 60% lower failure rate vs traditional development (Full Scale / industry aggregation)
- Time-to-market: 35% faster with MVP approach (Gartner cited in SDH, 2024)
- User insights: 72% of startups using MVP report better user insights (Gartner, 2024)
- Cost reduction: up to 60% vs full-build development

### The systematic mapping finding

A systematic mapping study of software engineering practices for MVP development (arxiv, 2305.08299, 2023) concluded:

> "Our analysis suggests that there are opportunities for solution proposals and evaluation studies to address literature gaps concerning technical feasibility assessment and effort estimation. Overall, more effort needs to be invested into empirically evaluating the existing MVP-related practices."

The research lags practice. MVP has stronger empirical support than MLP — but the empirical base for MVP methodology specifically is still developing.

### The Journal of Management contribution

Stevenson et al. (2024) published the first systematic theory-and-practice review of MVP as a phenomenological artefact. Classification: theoretical contribution, not an effectiveness trial. Conclusion: MVP practice has outpaced MVP research.

## MLP — the evidence assessment

### What MLP claims

Minimum Lovable Product: the smallest release that customers not only use but also genuinely enjoy, creating an emotional pull that drives early retention, advocacy, and pricing power. The MLP claim is that emotional attachment from day one produces better retention outcomes than validated-functional-first approaches.

### Origin

Brian de Haaff, Aha! co-founder and CEO, coined the term in 2013 and expanded it in *Lovability* (2017). The concept was introduced as a counterpoint to MVP, arguing that pursuing functional viability at the expense of emotional resonance produces products that users tolerate rather than advocate for.

### Evidence base

**Practitioner case studies:** Notion, Slack, Linear, Arc, Raycast. These products achieved strong early retention and word-of-mouth growth. All are cited as MLP examples.

**NPS logic:** MLP success is measured through Net Promoter Score, 30-day engagement, activation rates, and support burden per user. These are valid product metrics. They are not comparative trial results.

**What is absent:** No peer-reviewed comparative effectiveness study exists that demonstrates MLP-first approaches produce measurably better retention outcomes than MVP-first approaches followed by quality investment. The causal claim — emotional attachment from day one causes better outcomes than functional validation first — has not been tested in a controlled study.

### The honest finding

MLP is a practitioner construct with compelling logic and a strong case study base. It is empirically untested in any comparative study. Anyone citing MLP and MVP as equivalent evidence-backed approaches is overclaiming MLP.

## The correct comparative framing

| Dimension | MVP | MLP |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence base | Peer-reviewed (Management Science 2022) + practitioner aggregation | Practitioner case studies only |
| Primary purpose | Market demand validation before investment | Retention and emotional attachment from launch |
| Primary metric | Learning velocity, PMF signal, pivot decision quality | NPS, 30-day engagement, activation rate |
| Risk profile | Lower initial investment; faster failure on negative signals | Higher initial investment; wrong assumptions more expensive to correct |
| Best context | Unknown demand, high uncertainty, hypothesis-heavy environment | Known demand, competitive differentiation required, quality investment feasible |
| Empirical status | Supported for experimentation discipline and validation culture | No comparative effectiveness data |

## The connection to EA practice

The same core problem — measuring value before the investment is made, and responding to negative signals before commitment compounds — operates at the organisational architecture level.

Organisations invest in capabilities without validation (the BCM signal ignored). They decommission systems without integration mapping (the Integration Architecture not produced before the commitment is made). They transform processes without an as-is baseline (the BPM gap that produces failed to-be implementations).

MVP discipline applied to architectural decision-making is the mechanism VAF operationalises: validate the assumption the decision depends on before committing the investment. The Management Science finding — that validation culture improves performance 30–100% — applies to architectural decision-making as directly as to product development.

MLP discipline applied to EA is the argument that the artefact should not just function — it should be usable, readable, and designed for the person consuming it. A Business Capability Map heat map that communicates the investment gap clearly in 30 seconds is an MLP-equivalent EA output. A 40-page capability taxonomy is the MVP-equivalent that no-one reads.

Both arguments have value. Neither has a comparative effectiveness trial at the EA artefact level. That study does not exist.
