## Abstract

Australian IT governance frameworks are structurally mature but produce persistent execution gaps because verification mechanisms are not consistently embedded at the level where work happens. This working paper demonstrates that framework design is sound and internationally aligned — the problem is not policy absence. Verification mechanisms consistently sit above the altitude where execution occurs, producing the unresolved deficiency patterns documented across all jurisdictions in 2025–2026. Without structural change to how verification is embedded, new policies such as the DTA Whole-of-Government Cloud Computing Policy (effective July 2026) risk reproducing the same pattern.

By synthesising findings from the Australian National Audit Office (ANAO), jurisdictional Auditors-General, and the Deloitte Unify Review, this study identifies a recurring pattern of institutional decoupling between governance design and operational reality. The analysis maps these frameworks against major international standards — COBIT, TOGAF, ITIL 4, ISO/IEC 38500, and the NIST Cybersecurity Framework — using comparators selected for structural relevance rather than geographic proximity. It concludes that while Australian frameworks align closely with international standards in design, reported outcomes consistently highlight failures across three distinct stages: prevention, detection, and remediation of control gaps.

## 1. Introduction

The rapid digitalisation of public services has placed unprecedented demands on IT governance structures to deliver secure, efficient, and citizen-focused outcomes. In Australia, this responsibility is distributed across federal and state jurisdictions, creating a multi-layered governance landscape where central coordination intersects with localised implementation.

Federal mechanisms, led by the Digital Transformation Agency (DTA), emphasise whole-of-government oversight through the Digital and ICT Investment Oversight Framework (IOF) and the Australian Government Architecture (AGA). State jurisdictions have responded with varied models: Queensland's formalised enterprise architecture approach, New South Wales' integration of governance within broader digital and cyber strategies, and Victoria's emphasis on cloud-first principles and service-oriented cyber initiatives.

Despite these efforts, 2025–2026 audit reports document a consistent pattern of implementation challenges. The Queensland Audit Office reported 13 significant and 198 total IT control deficiencies, with 43% of prior-year issues unresolved. The Deloitte Unify Review highlighted significant operational gaps in a major child safety system. Similar patterns of control weaknesses and verification shortfalls appear in federal, New South Wales, and Victorian audits.

This working paper examines the documented purposes of these governance frameworks and contrasts them with the outcomes reported in official audits. The evidence indicates a consistent pattern of institutional decoupling — well-articulated governance design that does not consistently translate into sustained control effectiveness and verification. This pattern is examined through a prevention-detection-remediation framework informed by governance dimensions drawn from COBIT and NIST.

## 2. Institutional Frameworks: The Architecture of Intent

### 2.1 Federal Coordination and Oversight

At the Commonwealth level, governance is characterised by a whole-of-government philosophy led by the DTA. The Digital and ICT Investment Oversight Framework (IOF) serves as the primary mechanism for end-to-end lifecycle management of digital investments. Complementing this is the Australian Government Architecture (AGA), which functions as a meta-framework to ensure interoperability across business, data, and technology layers. The stated intent is to minimise redundancy, promote reuse, and maximise collective capability.

### 2.2 State-Level Adaptations

Queensland employs a highly structured Queensland Government Enterprise Architecture (QGEA), relying on Architecture Review Boards (ARBs) as formal quality gates and the QGEA Reference Group for coordination of data and AI standards. New South Wales and Victoria have adopted more integrated models, embedding IT governance within broader digital service delivery and cybersecurity policies. While Queensland's model emphasises structural compliance, the NSW and Victorian models prioritise agility and risk-based resilience.

### 2.3 Mapping to International Paradigms

Australian governance structures draw heavily from global standards. Elements of COBIT's governance and management objectives are evident in the DTA's investment oversight. The AGA and QGEA reflect aspects of TOGAF's Architecture Development Method. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework has become influential in state-level cyber governance, particularly in the Identify and Protect functions.

International comparators are selected here on structural grounds rather than geographic proximity. The UK Government Digital Service (GDS) is selected because it operates within a unitary rather than federated governance model, providing a direct structural contrast to Australia's advisory and federated design. Its mandatory service assessments function as hard verification gates — structurally different from Australia's ARB model in enforcement strength. Singapore GovTech is selected because it integrates strategy and execution under a single authority, eliminating the structural distance between governance design and delivery that characterises the Australian model. Canada — a federation with comparable governance complexity — is noted as a subject for further comparative research, as its audit outcomes would provide a more structurally precise peer comparison than unitary state models.

These comparators are not offered as models to replicate. They are offered as structural contrasts that illuminate the specific trade-offs of Australia's federated advisory design.

## 3. Methodology

This study employs qualitative documentary analysis and synthesis of official 2025–2026 artefacts. Purpose is defined as the stated objectives and mechanisms described in framework documents. Outcome refers to the findings and root causes explicitly reported in public audits. A prevention-detection-remediation mapping framework, informed by governance dimensions drawn from COBIT and NIST, structures the comparison. The approach remains neutral and limited to explicit content in the sources.

A key limitation is the differing audit methodologies across jurisdictions. Queensland reports deficiency counts, New South Wales high-risk findings, and Victoria targeted technical audits. Direct numerical comparison is therefore challenging, and findings are interpreted as indicative patterns rather than definitive rankings.

The study distinguishes between two categories of root cause: structural constraints — legacy systems, governance positioning, federated design — and organisational variables — staff turnover, competing priorities, third-party dependencies. The policy response to a structural constraint is repositioning of verification mechanisms. The policy response to an organisational variable is resourcing or process change. The evidence points primarily to structural constraints as the higher-leverage root cause, though organisational variables compound the effect. That prioritisation is explicit rather than assumed.

The research captures a snapshot during a period of transition. Some deficiencies may reflect temporary implementation lag rather than steady-state performance.

## 4. The Reality of Governance: 2025–2026 Evidence

### 4.1 The Federal Landscape: Persistent Control Risks

The ANAO 2025 financial statement audits identify the IT control environment as the most significant risk area, with approximately 45% of findings relating to IT controls. Many are unresolved from prior audits — a detection and remediation failure, not a design failure. The frameworks identify the requirement. They do not surface the gap or close it.

### 4.2 The Queensland Experience: The Unify Case and QAO Findings

The Queensland Audit Office Report 6 (2025–26) presents 13 significant deficiencies and 198 total IT control deficiencies, with 43% of prior-year issues unresolved. Root causes cited include competing priorities, staff turnover, organisational restructures, third-party governance challenges, and insufficient verification of control effectiveness.

The Deloitte Unify Review (December 2025) provides a detailed case study of the $183 million child safety case management system that went live in April 2025. Key findings include gaps in system design, functionality, and performance; difficulties for frontline staff in accessing critical information; a staff approval rating of 1.79 out of 10; and challenges in information exchange with partner agencies. A remediation team of approximately 120 staff was established.

The Unify case is a prevention failure at the assurance gate and a detection failure in operational readiness assessment. Formal governance processes operated correctly at their designed altitude. What they could not see — because they were not positioned to see it — was operational fitness at the delivery layer.

### 4.3 New South Wales and Victoria: Infrastructure and Risk Management Challenges

In New South Wales, the Auditor-General (October 2025) reported five high-risk IT findings across 26 major agencies, with approximately half involving IT controls over financial systems. Gaps in formal cyber security risk management covering supply chain risks were noted — a prevention failure at the policy design layer.

Victoria's VAGO report (29 October 2025) found that no examined agency maintained a complete and accurate server inventory. Agencies were described as flying blind on server security controls — a detection failure. The foundational visibility required to assess risk was absent. Every downstream governance activity operated on assumption rather than verified data.

## 5. Critical Synthesis and Comparative Analysis

The 2025–2026 audit evidence reveals a recurring pattern of institutional decoupling between the documented intent of governance frameworks and their reported operational outcomes. This decoupling is analysed across three distinct and non-overlapping failure stages.

**Prevention failures** occur when frameworks do not stop the gap from forming. The NSW supply chain risk management gap and the Unify assurance gate failure are prevention failures. The governance instrument was present. It was not positioned to prevent the outcome.

**Detection failures** occur when frameworks do not surface the gap once it exists. Victoria's server inventory finding is a detection failure. The visibility required to identify the risk was absent. Queensland's 43% unresolved rate contains detection failures where prior-year items were not surfaced for effective remediation.

**Remediation failures** occur when frameworks do not close the gap once it is detected. The persistent unresolved deficiency rate across all jurisdictions — carried from prior cycles — represents remediation failure. Detection occurred. Closure did not.

These three categories are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive. A gap either was not prevented, was not detected, or was not remediated. Addressing only one stage without the others will not resolve the pattern.

### Mapping Matrix: Prevention-Detection-Remediation Framework (2025–2026 Evidence)

| Failure Stage | Stated Purpose (Design) | Reported Outcome (Execution) | Observed Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prevention | Rigorous assurance gates (IOF/ARBs) | Unify: critical functionality gaps post-review | Gate not positioned for operational fitness |
| Detection | Integrated asset inventories (AGA/QGEA) | VAGO: agencies flying blind on server counts | Foundational visibility absent |
| Remediation | Continual improvement tracking (COBIT) | QAO: 43% of deficiencies unresolved | Verification not embedded at execution layer |

The causal mechanisms underlying these gaps are not equal in leverage. Legacy systems represent a structural constraint — they increase control complexity and limit automation options. Outsourcing and third-party dependencies represent a governance design choice that reduces visibility and direct accountability. Staff turnover and competing priorities represent organisational variables that compound structural problems but do not cause them independently.

The primary lever is structural positioning. If verification mechanisms were embedded at the altitude where execution happens, organisational variables would produce less persistent outcomes. The inverse is not true — resolving staff turnover without repositioning verification would not produce materially different audit findings.

When mapped against international frameworks, Australian governance design shows alignment with TOGAF's architecture methods and COBIT's governance objectives. The UK GDS mandatory service assessments and Singapore GovTech's integrated execution model represent higher enforcement strength and lower structural distance between governance and delivery — not because their frameworks are more sophisticated, but because their governance models do not carry Australia's federated advisory trade-off.

## 6. Discussion: Policy Developments and Future Directions

The DTA Whole-of-Government Cloud Computing Policy (effective July 2026) and the updated Responsible AI Policy represent meaningful steps toward addressing legacy risks and strengthening accountability. However, these policies operate at the same structural altitude as the frameworks they complement. If verification mechanisms remain above the altitude where execution happens, new policies will add administrative layers without resolving the underlying structural gap.

The reliance on third-party service providers is a governance design choice that consistently produces reduced visibility in audit findings. Both NSW and Queensland reports note difficulty in verifying control effectiveness when management is outsourced. This is not a vendor problem. It is a structural positioning problem — the verification mechanism is not embedded at the altitude where the third-party work occurs.

Future research should examine Canada as a structurally precise peer comparator — a federation navigating comparable governance complexity — and extend this analysis to New Zealand for a Trans-Tasman perspective on verification, cloud migration, and AI governance.

---

## 7. Conclusion

Australian IT governance frameworks are structurally mature and internationally aligned in design. The 2025–2026 audit evidence reveals a consistent pattern of failure across three distinct stages — prevention, detection, and remediation — that cannot be resolved by policy addition alone.

The primary root cause is structural: verification mechanisms are positioned above the altitude where execution happens. This is a higher-leverage explanation than organisational variables such as staff turnover or competing priorities, which compound the problem without causing it independently.

New policies will not resolve this pattern unless they change the structural positioning of verification. The 2026 Cloud Computing Policy creates the mandate. What determines whether that mandate produces different audit findings in 2027 is whether verification gets embedded closer to execution — or whether the policy sits at one altitude while the control environment operates at another.

A companion diagnostic piece examines the structural conditions that sustain this gap — and what it takes to close them in practice.
